clinical terminology without the clinical bullshit. these are terms youll see in research papers, evaluations, and discussions about ASPD. knowing what they actually mean helps you understand your own diagnosis and navigate the system.

A B C D E F G I L M N P R S

A

Affective Empathy

the ability to actually feel what someone else is feeling. its the emotional contagion part - when someone cries and you feel sad too. this is what most people with ASPD lack or have reduced. different from cognitive empathy.

you can understand someone is sad without feeling sad yourself. thats cognitive empathy without affective empathy.

Antisocial Personality Disorder (ASPD)

the clinical diagnosis in the DSM-5. characterized by a pervasive pattern of disregard for and violation of the rights of others, beginning in childhood/adolescence and continuing into adulthood. requires evidence of conduct disorder before age 15 and being at least 18 to diagnose.

not the same as being antisocial (introverted). the "antisocial" here means against society/social norms.

Amygdala

part of the brain involved in processing emotions, especially fear and aggression. research shows people with ASPD often have reduced amygdala activity and sometimes reduced amygdala volume. this is part of why fear-based deterrents dont work as well on us.

B

Boldness

one of the three factors in the triarchic model of psychopathy. refers to social dominance, low fear, and stress immunity. its the "fearless confidence" aspect. some researchers argue this is actually adaptive and not inherently pathological.

C

Callous-Unemotional Traits (CU Traits)

a set of traits in children that often predict ASPD. includes lack of guilt, lack of empathy, shallow emotions, and not caring about performance. used to identify kids at risk before they can be diagnosed with ASPD (which requires being 18+).

Cluster B

the category of personality disorders in the DSM that includes ASPD, borderline (BPD), narcissistic (NPD), and histrionic personality disorders. these are grouped together because they all involve dramatic, emotional, or erratic behavior patterns.

comorbidity between cluster B disorders is common. having ASPD with narcissistic traits, for example, is very frequent.

Cognitive Empathy

the ability to understand what someone else is thinking or feeling without necessarily feeling it yourself. its intellectual understanding of emotions. most people with ASPD have intact or even enhanced cognitive empathy - we can read people well, we just dont feel what they feel.

Comorbidity

having more than one diagnosis at the same time. ASPD commonly co-occurs with substance use disorders, ADHD, depression, other personality disorders (especially NPD), and anxiety disorders.

Conduct Disorder (CD)

the childhood/adolescent precursor to ASPD. you cannot be diagnosed with ASPD unless there was evidence of conduct disorder before age 15. includes aggression, destruction of property, deceitfulness, and serious rule violations.

not everyone with CD develops ASPD, but ASPD requires a history of CD.

D

Disinhibition

one of the three factors in the triarchic model. refers to poor impulse control, difficulty with planning, and need for immediate gratification. this is the "acting without thinking" aspect that causes a lot of practical problems.

DSM-5

the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th edition. this is what clinicians in the US use to diagnose ASPD. the criteria are different from the PCL-R and focus more on behavioral patterns than personality traits.

Duping Delight

the pleasure or satisfaction some people get from successfully deceiving others. that little rush when you pull something off and no one catches on. not a clinical term but commonly discussed in ASPD contexts.

E

Egosyntonic

when something feels consistent with your sense of self and doesnt bother you. ASPD traits are typically egosyntonic - we dont experience them as problems that need fixing. this is why treatment is difficult: theres often no internal motivation to change.

opposite of egodystonic, where traits feel foreign and distressing (like with OCD intrusive thoughts).

F

Factor 1 / Factor 2

the two main components of psychopathy as measured by the PCL-R. Factor 1 is the interpersonal/affective stuff (superficial charm, lack of empathy, manipulation). Factor 2 is the lifestyle/antisocial stuff (impulsivity, irresponsibility, criminal behavior).

some people score high on one factor but not the other. "successful psychopaths" often have high Factor 1 but lower Factor 2.

Flat Affect

reduced emotional expression. showing fewer facial expressions, vocal variations, or body language that indicates emotion. common in ASPD. doesnt mean you feel nothing - just means it doesnt show externally as much.

G

Grandiosity

an inflated sense of self-importance. believing youre smarter, more capable, or more important than others. overlaps with narcissism and is common in ASPD. can be realistic (actually being competent) or delusional.

I

ICD-11

the International Classification of Diseases, 11th revision. used more in Europe and internationally. renamed ASPD to "Dissocial Personality Disorder" and uses a dimensional model. focuses on patterns of behavior affecting others negatively.

Impulse Control Disorder

difficulty controlling urges or impulses even when they lead to negative consequences. a core feature of ASPD. related to the prefrontal cortex, which handles executive function and is often underactive in ASPD.

Instrumental Aggression

aggression used as a tool to achieve a goal (getting money, establishing dominance, etc.) versus reactive aggression which is emotional response to provocation. ASPD is associated with higher instrumental aggression.

L

Low Arousal Theory

the theory that people with ASPD have chronically low nervous system arousal and seek stimulation to reach normal levels. explains risk-taking, sensation-seeking, and why boredom is such a problem for us.

M

Machiavellian

characterized by manipulation, exploitation, and disregard for morality to achieve goals. part of the "dark triad" along with narcissism and psychopathy. named after Niccolò Machiavelli who wrote about political manipulation.

Mask / Masking

presenting a false front to appear more normal or socially acceptable. pretending to have emotions or care about things you dont. most people with ASPD learn to mask early. exhausting but often necessary.

Meanness

one of the three factors in the triarchic model. refers to callousness, lack of empathy, exploitation of others, and cruel behavior. the "cold" aspect of psychopathy.

N

Neurotypical (NT)

people with typical neurological development. used to describe people without ASPD or other conditions that affect brain function. the "normal" baseline that society is designed around.

P

PCL-R (Psychopathy Checklist-Revised)

the gold standard assessment for psychopathy, created by Robert Hare. 20-item checklist scored by a trained clinician. scores range from 0-40. cutoff of 30+ is typically used for psychopathy diagnosis in North America (25+ in UK/Europe).

ASPD and psychopathy arent the same thing. most people with psychopathy meet ASPD criteria, but not everyone with ASPD meets psychopathy criteria.

Primary vs Secondary Psychopathy

primary psychopathy is thought to be more innate/genetic - characterized by emotional deficits, low anxiety, and calculated behavior. secondary psychopathy is more associated with environmental factors, higher anxiety, and reactive behavior. research suggests different underlying mechanisms.

Prefrontal Cortex

the front part of the brain responsible for executive functions: planning, decision-making, impulse control, and considering consequences. often shows reduced activity or structural differences in people with ASPD.

Prosocial Behavior

voluntary behavior intended to benefit others. the opposite of antisocial behavior. can be genuine or strategic - doing good things because it benefits you is still prosocial behavior, just differently motivated.

Psychopathy

a personality construct characterized by persistent antisocial behavior, impaired empathy/remorse, and bold/disinhibited/egotistical traits. measured by PCL-R. overlaps with but is not identical to ASPD. more focused on personality traits than just behavior.

R

Recidivism

reoffending after being released from prison or completing a sentence. ASPD is associated with higher recidivism rates, which is why its often assessed in forensic settings. the whole "will they do it again" evaluation.

Remorse

feeling bad about something you did because of the harm it caused others. different from regret (wishing you hadnt done something because of consequences to yourself). reduced or absent remorse is a core feature of ASPD.

S

Shallow Affect

experiencing emotions at a reduced intensity or depth. emotions might be brief, superficial, or just not as strong as others describe. one of the PCL-R items. doesnt mean no emotions - just different emotional experience.

Sociopathy

not a clinical term anymore but still commonly used. historically meant the same as psychopathy. some use it to distinguish environmentally-caused antisocial patterns (sociopathy) from innate ones (psychopathy) but this isnt a formal distinction.

Successful Psychopath

someone who meets criteria for psychopathy but hasnt been caught, convicted, or had their life significantly impaired. often high functioning in careers like business, law, surgery, etc. controversial concept but studied in research.

Superficial Charm

the ability to be likeable, engaging, and charming on the surface, often without genuine emotional connection. a PCL-R item. useful for manipulation but also just for social navigation.
